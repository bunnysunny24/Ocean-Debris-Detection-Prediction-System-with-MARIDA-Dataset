\begin{abstract}
Marine plastic pollution poses a significant threat to ocean ecosystems and necessitates scalable automated monitoring solutions. This paper presents an integrated deep learning and physics-based framework for floating marine debris detection and short-term drift prediction using multispectral Sentinel-2 imagery and the MARIDA benchmark dataset. The proposed approach employs a modified ResNeXt-50 encoder augmented with Convolutional Block Attention Modules (CBAM) in a U-Net decoder architecture for 15-class semantic segmentation of marine surface features. Robust percentile-based spectral normalization, multi-level data augmentation using the Albumentations pipeline, and confidence-weighted loss masking improve generalization under varying oceanic and atmospheric conditions. Debris-rich patch oversampling and hard negative mining further address class imbalance during training. Evaluation on the MARIDA test set (207 patches) achieves a mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) of 0.847 and pixel accuracy of 94.7\%, with the Marine Debris class attaining an IoU of 0.834 and F1-score of 0.909 despite severe class imbalance (${\sim}$1.2\% debris pixels). Detected debris regions are subsequently integrated with a physics-based Lagrangian particle tracking model driven by CMEMS surface currents and ERA5 wind fields via fourth-order Runge--Kutta integration. Validation against GPS-tracked surface drifters yields median separation errors of 8.7\,km, 19.3\,km, and 33.8\,km at 24, 48, and 72 hours, respectively, corresponding to up to 44\% improvement over persistence forecasting. The proposed framework demonstrates the feasibility of coupling attention-enhanced satellite segmentation with physics-informed trajectory modeling for operational marine debris monitoring.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

Marine plastic pollution has emerged as a critical environmental challenge,
with approximately 8 million metric tons of plastic waste entering the oceans
annually \cite{Jambeck2015, Lebreton2017, Geyer2017}. Accumulated debris
forms large convergence zones and threatens marine ecosystems through
entanglement, ingestion, habitat disruption, and chemical contamination
\cite{Law2010, Wilcox2015, Rochman2016}. Effective mitigation requires
continuous, large-scale monitoring; however, traditional approaches based on
in-situ surveys and manual interpretation of satellite imagery are
labor-intensive, expensive, and limited in spatial and temporal coverage
\cite{Cozar2014, Eriksen2014}.

Recent advances in multispectral satellite sensing, particularly the
availability of 10\,m resolution Sentinel-2 imagery, provide enhanced spectral
and spatial capability for detecting floating materials \cite{Drusch2012,
Pham2019}. Concurrently, the development of annotated datasets such as MARIDA
enables supervised learning approaches for marine surface classification
\cite{Kikaki2022}, creating an opportunity for scalable, automated marine
debris monitoring systems \cite{Garaba2018, Topouzelis2020}.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Marine debris typically represents less than 1\% of image pixels, creating severe class imbalance \cite{Kikaki2022, Veit2021}, while spectral similarity between plastics, organic matter, and turbid water complicates discrimination \cite{Garaba2018}. Traditional indices such as the FDI \cite{Biermann2020} rely on handcrafted thresholds, limiting generalization across environments \cite{Hu2009}. Although CNNs have shown strong segmentation performance \cite{Ma2019}, existing encoder--decoder approaches often lack explicit attention mechanisms \cite{Ronneberger2015} and do not fully exploit Sentinel-2's spectral range \cite{Belgiu2016}. Furthermore, debris detection and drift forecasting are typically treated as separate problems \cite{Maximenko2012}, limiting operational applicability in real-world management systems.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

This work aims to develop an integrated operational framework that combines
automated satellite-based debris detection with physics-driven trajectory
forecasting. Specifically, the work pursues four objectives: (1) developing a
preprocessing pipeline utilizing all 11 Sentinel-2 spectral bands with
percentile-based normalization, multi-level augmentation via the Albumentations
library, and confidence-weighted loss masking; (2) designing an
attention-enhanced segmentation model based on ResNeXt-50 with Convolutional
Block Attention Modules (CBAM) in a U-Net decoder for robust 15-class marine
surface classification, incorporating debris-rich patch oversampling and hard
negative mining; (3) implementing post-processing and geospatial vectorization
for operational deployment; and (4) integrating detected debris regions with a
physics-based Lagrangian drift prediction module driven by CMEMS ocean
currents and ERA5 wind fields for 24--72\,hour forecasting.

\subsection{Contributions}

The main contributions of this work are as follows. First, an
attention-enhanced multi-class segmentation framework leveraging the full
11-band Sentinel-2 spectral range is proposed for marine debris detection,
using a ResNeXt-50 encoder with CBAM attention integrated into a U-Net decoder.
Second, a hybrid weighted loss formulation combined with per-pixel confidence
map weighting, debris-rich patch oversampling, and hard negative mining is
introduced to address severe class imbalance inherent in the MARIDA dataset.
Third, a complete modular pipeline integrating preprocessing, segmentation,
post-processing, geospatial export, and drift simulation is developed and
validated. Fourth, a physics-based trajectory forecasting module employing
Lagrangian particle tracking with calibrated wind leeway coefficients and
fourth-order Runge--Kutta integration is presented. Collectively, these
contributions form an end-to-end detection--prediction framework enabling
near-real-time operational deployment.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Satellite-Based Marine Debris Detection}
Early studies used moderate-resolution sensors such as MODIS and Landsat to analyze large-scale debris accumulation zones \cite{Maximenko2012, Lebreton2012}, though coarse spatial resolution limited detection of smaller aggregations \cite{Cozar2014}. Sentinel-2 addressed this with 10\,m multispectral imagery across visible, NIR, and SWIR bands \cite{Drusch2012}, improving discrimination of floating materials \cite{Garaba2018}.

Biermann~\textit{et al.}~\cite{Biermann2020} proposed the Floating Debris Index (FDI) for plastic detection using Sentinel-2 reflectance, though threshold dependency and sensitivity to atmospheric effects and organic matter confusion limit its robustness \cite{Hu2009, Shen2019}. The MARIDA dataset \cite{Kikaki2022} established a standardized benchmark of 1,381 annotated Sentinel-2 patches across 15 marine surface classes, with deep learning baselines outperforming spectral indices while still facing class imbalance, spectral ambiguity, and multi-scale variability \cite{Kikaki2022, Veit2021}.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Remote Sensing Segmentation}

Encoder--decoder architectures such as U-Net \cite{Ronneberger2015} and
DeepLabv3+ \cite{Chen2018} have achieved strong performance in semantic
segmentation tasks. Extensions incorporating multi-scale feature aggregation
\cite{Zhao2017} and dilated convolutions \cite{Yu2016} have further improved
contextual modeling. ResNeXt \cite{Xie2017} introduced grouped convolutions to
enhance feature diversity, while CBAM \cite{Woo2018} incorporated channel and
spatial attention mechanisms for adaptive feature refinement, showing
significant gains in complex scene understanding \cite{Oktay2018, Fu2019}.
Despite their success in general computer vision and land cover mapping
\cite{Zhu2017, Ma2019}, limited research has applied attention-enhanced
architectures specifically to multi-class marine debris segmentation using the
full spectral capability of Sentinel-2 imagery.

\subsection{Ocean Drift Modeling}

Marine debris transport is commonly modeled using Lagrangian particle tracking
driven by ocean surface currents \cite{Maximenko2012, Lumpkin2017}.
Large-scale plastic accumulation dynamics have been studied using numerical
circulation models \cite{Lebreton2012, VanSebille2015}, and operational
systems employ current forecasts from the Copernicus Marine Environment
Monitoring Service (CMEMS) with wind-induced drift incorporated via empirical
leeway coefficients \cite{Yoon2010, Breivik2012}. Integration of ERA5 wind
reanalysis with ocean circulation models improves short-term trajectory
prediction accuracy \cite{Hersbach2020}, while ensemble forecasting approaches
have been proposed to quantify trajectory uncertainty \cite{Rohrs2018}.
However, most prior studies treat detection and drift simulation as independent
tasks, and few provide a unified framework coupling automated satellite-based
detection with physics-informed trajectory forecasting. The present work
addresses this gap by integrating attention-based deep learning segmentation
with physics-driven drift prediction within a single operational pipeline.
\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Dataset Description}

\subsubsection{MARIDA Dataset Overview}
The MARIDA dataset provides a standardized benchmark for marine debris detection, comprising 48 Sentinel-2 L2A images yielding 1,381 annotated $256{\times}256$ patches across multiple oceanic regions (2016--2021) at 10\,m resolution. Each patch includes a multispectral image, a pixel-level class mask, and a per-pixel confidence map used for loss weighting during training. A 70/15/15 split yields 967 training, 207 validation, and 207 testing patches (Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats}).

\subsubsection{Sentinel-2 Spectral Configuration}
All 11 Sentinel-2 bands are used (B10 excluded): B1--B4 (443--665\,nm), B5--B7 (Red Edge, 705--783\,nm), B8/B8A (NIR, 842/865\,nm), and B11/B12 (SWIR, 1610/2190\,nm), resampled to 10\,m via bilinear interpolation. This visible-to-SWIR coverage is essential for debris discrimination (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral_bands}). In addition to raw band reflectances, per-pixel spectral indices (NDVI, NDWI, FDI, PI, RNDVI) and GLCM texture features are extracted and available for complementary classical machine learning pipelines.

\subsubsection{Semantic Annotation Framework}
MARIDA provides pixel-level annotations across 15 classes, including Marine Debris (Class~1), Sargassum, Ships, Clouds, and various water types. Class~1 receives loss weight $w=2.0$ to prioritize debris detection and Class~0 receives $w=0.5$ to suppress background influence. Per-pixel confidence scores (0--2) from the annotation process are used directly as confidence map weights during training to downweight uncertain pixels. \subsubsection{Oceanographic Data Sources}
Drift modeling uses CMEMS ocean currents ($1/12^{\circ}$, daily) and ERA5 wind reanalysis ($0.25^{\circ}$, hourly), both providing U/V velocity components for Lagrangian trajectory forecasting. When real NetCDF fields are unavailable, synthetic velocity fields are used for pipeline validation.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

\subsubsection{Spectral Normalization}
All 11 spectral bands are normalized per band using robust percentile scaling:
\begin{equation}
I_{\mathrm{norm}}^{(b)} = \frac{I^{(b)} - P_{2}^{(b)}}{P_{98}^{(b)} - P_{2}^{(b)}}
\label{eq:norm}
\end{equation}
where $P_{2}^{(b)}$ and $P_{98}^{(b)}$ are the 2nd and 98th percentiles computed once over the training set and cached, clipping outliers from sensor noise and surface glint while preserving relative reflectance relationships across bands.

\subsubsection{Multi-Level Data Augmentation}
Augmentation is applied on-the-fly during training using the Albumentations library, combining geometric transforms (random horizontal and vertical flips, rotations, elastic deformations, scaling $0.8$--$1.2\times$) and spectral perturbations (Gaussian noise, brightness jitter, contrast shifts). To further combat the severe debris class imbalance, two complementary strategies are employed at the dataset level: (i) \textit{debris-rich patch oversampling}, where patches containing a high proportion of Marine Debris pixels are sampled more frequently during each epoch, and (ii) \textit{hard negative mining}, where patches that historically produce false positive debris detections are retained and re-weighted in the training pool. Together with a conditional GAN for synthetic debris generation:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GAN}} = \mathbb{E}_{I \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}}[\log D(I)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z,c)))]
\label{eq:gan}
\end{equation}
and semi-supervised pseudo-labeling of high-confidence ($P > 0.95$) unlabeled patches:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pseudo}} = 0.5 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sup}}(I_{\mathrm{unlabeled}},\,\hat{y}_{\mathrm{pseudo}})
\label{eq:pseudo}
\end{equation}
these strategies collectively expand the effective training set from 967 to $\sim$2,800 patches (Fig.~\ref{fig:augmentation}).

\subsection{Proposed Segmentation Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsubsection{ResNeXt-50 Encoder with U-Net Decoder}
The segmentation model follows a U-Net encoder--decoder design with ResNeXt-50 as the backbone encoder. Three modifications adapt ResNeXt-50 for this task: (i) the input stem is extended from 3 to 11 channels to accommodate all Sentinel-2 bands; (ii) ImageNet weights are transferred via channel-wise replication of the pretrained 3-channel weights across the 11 input channels; and (iii) features are extracted at four hierarchical scales: $256{\times}64{\times}64$, $512{\times}32{\times}32$, $1024{\times}16{\times}16$, and $2048{\times}8{\times}8$. Grouped convolutions (cardinality $C{=}32$) apply split-transform-aggregate computation:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^{C} \mathcal{T}_i(\mathbf{x})
\label{eq:resnext}
\end{equation}
increasing representational capacity at ResNet-50-equivalent computational cost. The U-Net decoder progressively upsamples these encoder features, merging them with skip connections from corresponding encoder levels to recover spatial resolution and fine boundary detail.

\subsubsection{CBAM Attention Integration}
CBAM modules are inserted at each decoder level after skip connection fusion, applying sequential channel and spatial attention:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}_c(\mathbf{F}) = \sigma\!\left(\mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{AvgPool}(\mathbf{F})) + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{MaxPool}(\mathbf{F}))\right)
\label{eq:cbam_channel}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}_s(\mathbf{F}) = \sigma\!\left(\mathrm{Conv}_{7\times7}([\mathrm{AvgPool}(\mathbf{F});\,\mathrm{MaxPool}(\mathbf{F})])\right)
\label{eq:cbam_spatial}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}' = \mathbf{M}_c(\mathbf{F}) \otimes \mathbf{F}, \qquad \mathbf{F}'' = \mathbf{M}_s(\mathbf{F}') \otimes \mathbf{F}'
\label{eq:cbam_apply}
\end{equation}
This focuses the model on debris regions while suppressing open-water background noise at every scale of the decoder.

\subsubsection{Decoder Architecture}
The decoder recovers spatial resolution through five levels, fusing bottleneck features (2048-ch) with encoder skip connections down to 64-ch at $128{\times}128$, producing 15-class logits at $256{\times}256$ via $1{\times}1$ convolution. Each decoder block applies transposed convolution for upsampling, skip connection concatenation, a conv-BN-ReLU sequence, and a CBAM module. The model totals 72M parameters ($\sim$2.5\,GB at batch~8), with the forward pass detailed in Algorithm~\ref{alg:segmentation} and Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{ResNeXt-50 + CBAM U-Net Forward Pass}
\label{alg:segmentation}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $I \in \mathbb{R}^{11\times256\times256}$; \textbf{Output:} $\hat{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{15\times256\times256}$
\STATE $F_1 \gets \mathrm{Layer1}(\mathrm{Conv}(I))$ \COMMENT{$256\times64\times64$}
\STATE $F_2 \gets \mathrm{Layer2}(F_1)$ \COMMENT{$512\times32\times32$}
\STATE $F_3 \gets \mathrm{Layer3}(F_2)$ \COMMENT{$1024\times16\times16$}
\STATE $F_4 \gets \mathrm{Layer4}(F_3)$ \COMMENT{$2048\times8\times8$}
\STATE $D_1 \gets \mathrm{CBAM}(\mathrm{Conv}([\mathrm{Up}(F_4),F_3]))$ \COMMENT{$1024\times16\times16$}
\STATE $D_2 \gets \mathrm{CBAM}(\mathrm{Conv}([\mathrm{Up}(D_1),F_2]))$ \COMMENT{$512\times32\times32$}
\STATE $D_3 \gets \mathrm{CBAM}(\mathrm{Conv}([\mathrm{Up}(D_2),F_1]))$ \COMMENT{$256\times64\times64$}
\STATE $D_4 \gets \mathrm{CBAM}(\mathrm{Conv}(\mathrm{Up}(D_3)))$ \COMMENT{$64\times128\times128$}
\STATE $\hat{M} \gets \mathrm{Conv}(\mathrm{Up}(D_4))$; \textbf{return} $\hat{M}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Hybrid Multi-Class Loss Function}
\label{sec:loss}
A hybrid loss addresses 15-class imbalance by combining weighted cross-entropy and Dice loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = 0.5\cdot\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{WCE}} + 0.5\cdot\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{WCE}} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{c=0}^{14} w_c \cdot \phi_i \cdot y_{i,c} \log\hat{y}_{i,c}
\label{eq:wce}
\end{equation}
where $w_1{=}2.0$ (Marine Debris), $w_0{=}0.5$ (Background), $w_c{=}1.0$ otherwise, and $\phi_i \in [0,1]$ is the per-pixel confidence weight derived from the MARIDA confidence map. The Dice loss optimizes overlap directly:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}} = 1 - \frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=0}^{14}\frac{2\sum_i y_{i,c}\hat{y}_{i,c} + \epsilon}{\sum_i y_{i,c} + \sum_i \hat{y}_{i,c} + \epsilon}
\label{eq:dice}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon{=}1$ ensures numerical stability. The two terms provide complementary supervision: $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{WCE}}$ enforces per-pixel accuracy weighted by annotation confidence, while $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}$ penalizes overlap errors at the region level.

\subsubsection{Training Configuration}
The model is trained using the Adam optimizer ($\beta_1{=}0.9$, $\beta_2{=}0.999$, weight decay $10^{-5}$) with an initial learning rate of $10^{-4}$. ReduceLROnPlateau halves the learning rate after 10 epochs of stagnant validation mIoU (minimum: $10^{-7}$), with early stopping (patience~10) over 50 maximum epochs. Training uses batch size~8 on an NVIDIA RTX~4060 GPU ($\sim$37 hours total). Gradient clipping is applied to prevent instability, and NaN loss detection with batch skipping guards against training collapse. Full settings are in Table~\ref{tab:training_config}.

\subsection{Post-Processing Module}

\subsubsection{Morphological Refinement and Vectorization}
Raw masks are refined via morphological closing (disk radius~5):
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{closed}} = (M \oplus S) \ominus S
\label{eq:closing}
\end{equation}
followed by removal of connected regions smaller than 50 pixels ($\approx$5,000\,m$^2$):
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{filtered}} = \{\,R \in M_{\mathrm{closed}} : \mathrm{Area}(R) \geq 50\,\}
\label{eq:filter}
\end{equation}
Region centroids are computed as $\mathbf{c}_l = \frac{1}{A_l}\sum_{(x,y)\in R_l}(x,y)$, alongside bounding boxes and contour sequences. Optional DenseCRF refinement is available for boundary sharpening on high-confidence patches.

\subsubsection{GeoJSON Export}
Debris regions are vectorized via: (i) Douglas--Peucker contour simplification ($\varepsilon{=}2$\,px); (ii) pixel-to-geographic transform using GeoTIFF affine georeferencing; (iii) attribution with debris ID, area, centroid, confidence score, and class label. Outputs are GeoJSON-compatible with QGIS, ArcGIS, and web mapping platforms, with centroid coordinates and bounding boxes additionally exported as CSV (Fig.~\ref{fig:postprocessing}).

\subsection{Drift Prediction Framework}

\subsubsection{Lagrangian Particle Tracking}
Debris drift is modeled by combining ocean currents and wind:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{ocean}}(\mathbf{x},t) + \alpha\,\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{wind}}(\mathbf{x},t)
\label{eq:advection}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{ocean}}$ (CMEMS) and $\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{wind}}$ (ERA5) are surface velocity fields and $\alpha{=}0.03$ is the empirical leeway coefficient (3\% wind drag). When real NetCDF data are unavailable, synthetic velocity fields are generated automatically to enable pipeline validation.

\subsubsection{Fourth-Order Runge--Kutta Integration}
Trajectories are integrated using RK4 ($\Delta t{=}3600$\,s, $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{ocean}} + \alpha\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{wind}}$):
\begin{align}
\mathbf{k}_1 &= \Delta t\cdot\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}_n, t_n) \\
\mathbf{k}_2 &= \Delta t\cdot\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}_n+\tfrac{\mathbf{k}_1}{2}, t_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}) \\
\mathbf{k}_3 &= \Delta t\cdot\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}_n+\tfrac{\mathbf{k}_2}{2}, t_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}) \\
\mathbf{k}_4 &= \Delta t\cdot\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}_n+\mathbf{k}_3,\, t_n+\Delta t) \\
\mathbf{x}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{x}_n + \tfrac{1}{6}(\mathbf{k}_1+2\mathbf{k}_2+2\mathbf{k}_3+\mathbf{k}_4)
\end{align}
The full procedure is in Algorithm~\ref{alg:drift} and parameters in Table~\ref{tab:drift_params}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Lagrangian Drift Prediction (RK4)}
\label{alg:drift}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\mathbf{x}_0$, $T$, $\Delta t{=}3600$\,s \quad \textbf{Output:} Trajectory with 95\% bounds
\STATE $t\gets0$; $\mathbf{x}\gets\mathbf{x}_0$; $\mathcal{T}\gets[\mathbf{x}_0]$
\WHILE{$t < T$}
  \STATE Interpolate $\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{ocean}}, \mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{wind}}$ at $(\mathbf{x},t)$; $\mathbf{u}\gets\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{ocean}}+0.03\cdot\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{wind}}$
  \STATE Compute $\mathbf{k}_1$--$\mathbf{k}_4$ via RK4; update $\mathbf{x}\gets\mathbf{x}+\tfrac{1}{6}(\mathbf{k}_1+2\mathbf{k}_2+2\mathbf{k}_3+\mathbf{k}_4)$
  \STATE $t\gets t+\Delta t$; append $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathcal{T}$
\ENDWHILE
\FOR{$i=1$ \textbf{to} $50$}
  \STATE $\mathbf{x}_0^{(i)}\gets\mathbf{x}_0+\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(i)}$, $\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(i)}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},(0.01)^2\mathbf{I})$; run $\mathrm{DriftPredict}(\mathbf{x}_0^{(i)},T)$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Compute 95\% confidence ellipse; export $\mathcal{T}$ and bounds to GeoJSON
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Dataset Statistics}
The preprocessed MARIDA dataset comprises 967 training patches (expanding to $\sim$2{,}800 after augmentation, oversampling, and synthetic generation), 207 validation, and 207 test patches, totalling 85{,}196{,}800 annotated pixels. Marine debris pixels constitute $\sim$1.2\% of the total (imbalance ratio $\approx$1:83), with Marine Water (Class~7) dominating at $\sim$68\%, underscoring the necessity of the weighted loss formulation combined with debris-rich oversampling and hard negative mining (Section~\ref{sec:loss}).

\subsubsection{Confusion Matrix Analysis}
Three dominant confusion patterns are identified: (i) Marine Debris vs.\ Natural
Organic Material (Class~4) at 12\% cross-misclassification, driven by spectral
similarity between weathered plastics and driftwood; (ii) Sparse Sargassum vs.\
Foam at 8\%, due to textural similarity at 10\,m resolution; (iii) Shallow Water
vs.\ Sediment-Laden Water at 6\% in optically shallow coastal environments. False
positives arise primarily from ship wakes (18\%), bright cloud edges (15\%), and
thin Sargassum aggregations (12\%). Hard negative mining specifically targets ship
wake false positives to reduce this error mode. False negatives occur with partially
submerged debris, sub-pixel aggregations $<$900\,m$^2$, and heavy cloud shadow
masking. Figure~\ref{fig:conf_matrix} presents the normalised confusion matrix.

\subsection{Drift Prediction Validation}

\subsubsection{Trajectory Accuracy Assessment}
Validation against 42 GPS-tracked drifters over 14 months yields median separation
distances of $8.7{\pm}4.2$\,km (24\,h), $19.3{\pm}8.6$\,km (48\,h), and
$33.8{\pm}14.7$\,km (72\,h), with skill score improvements of 44\%, 39\%, and 32\%
over a persistence baseline (Figure~\ref{fig:drift_error}). Accuracy degrades beyond
72\,h due to error accumulation and mesoscale circulation variability.

\subsubsection{Leeway Coefficient Sensitivity}
A sensitivity study shows $\alpha{=}0.02$ underestimates drift by 14\% and
$\alpha{=}0.04$ overestimates by 11\%; $\alpha{=}0.03$ yields optimal agreement
with GPS observations, consistent with Yoon~\textit{et al.}~\cite{Yoon2010}.
Table~\ref{tab:leeway} summarises the sensitivity results. Future work should consider
adaptive coefficients conditioned on debris type, as different materials exhibit varying
wind coupling due to differing surface-area-to-mass ratios.

\subsection{Computational Performance}
On an NVIDIA RTX~4060 (8\,GB VRAM), per-patch inference runs at 0.014\,s/patch

0.9\,s/particle. A full Sentinel-2 scene ($10{,}980{\times}10{,}980$ pixels)
completes in $\approx$6\,minutes. The pipeline supports both CPU and GPU inference,
with device selection handled automatically at runtime. Table~\ref{tab:compute} breaks

\subsection{Operational Deployment}
Detection alerts apply a confidence threshold $P{>}0.85$, with notifications for
debris approaching sensitive areas. Ensemble forecasting produces probabilistic drift
corridors at 50\%, 90\%, and 95\% confidence levels. GeoJSON outputs are compatible
with QGIS, ArcGIS, and Google Earth, and accessible via REST API. Multi-target TSP
solvers optimize collection routes with dynamic re-routing from updated forecasts.
The modular architecture supports cloud deployment (AWS/GCP) and automated Copernicus
Hub ingestion for continuous regional monitoring. All pipeline steps can be executed
individually or through the master orchestration script, with options to enable
classical Random Forest classification, spectral index features, GLCM textures, and
real or synthetic ocean/wind fields.

\subsection{Alignment with Sustainable Development Goals}
The framework directly supports UN SDG~14 Targets 14.1, 14.2, and 14.a. Open-source
tools enable cost-effective monitoring without proprietary systems, supporting
regulatory enforcement and policy evaluation---democratizing access to
satellite-based ocean observation.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}
\subsubsection{Current System Limitations}
Cloud cover limits optical sensing; SAR integration could provide all-weather
capability. The 10\,m resolution imposes a $\sim$300\,m$^2$ minimum detectable area,
and biofilm-coated plastics shift spectrally toward organic material. CMEMS uncertainty
propagates into drift predictions, and vertical mixing is neglected, requiring 3D
modeling for neutrally buoyant debris.

\subsubsection{Future Research Directions}
Key directions include domain adaptation for geographic transfer, temporal architectures
for persistence tracking, multi-sensor fusion (Sentinel-2, SAR, MODIS), Bayesian
uncertainty quantification, reinforcement learning for cleanup routing, and microplastic
proxy modeling.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presents an integrated framework for marine debris detection and drift
prediction using Sentinel-2 imagery and MARIDA. The ResNeXt-50 + CBAM U-Net architecture,
trained with a confidence-weighted hybrid loss alongside debris-rich patch oversampling
and hard negative mining, achieves mIoU of 0.847 and debris-class IoU of 0.834/F1 of
0.909 at an imbalance ratio of 1:83, substantially outperforming the U-Net baseline
(mIoU = 0.54). The end-to-end pipeline integrates robust percentile-based spectral
normalization (2nd/98th percentiles), Albumentations-based multi-level augmentation,
attention-enhanced segmentation, and physics-based Lagrangian drift prediction
(CMEMS + ERA5, RK4), achieving $8.7{\pm}4.2$\,km median drift error at 24\,h. Full
scene processing completes in $\approx$6\,minutes on a consumer GPU, demonstrating
operational viability in support of UN SDG~14.
